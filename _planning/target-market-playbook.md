# DesignTransform / BonFX — Target Market & Playbook Background

---

## Who We're Targeting

Small creative shops, web agencies, and digital studios doing real production work. Not the "agency bro" culture posting about systems and scaling on Twitter. The people who are too busy doing the work to talk about doing the work.

### Profile

- Running 30-80 WordPress sites for clients
- Doing print work — business cards, brochures, signage, PDFs
- 1-5 person shop, maybe a couple freelancers on call
- Revenue comes from maintenance retainers, small project work, repeat clients
- Backlog is chronic — always behind, never caught up
- Owner is the bottleneck — designing, developing, emailing, invoicing
- Not anti-AI but not actively adopting it. Don't have time to learn new tools.
- Not on Twitter/X. Not watching YouTube tutorials about n8n or agents.

### Where They Actually Are

- Local business networking groups (BNI, chambers of commerce)
- WordPress meetups and WordCamps
- Facebook groups for their CMS or niche (Elementor, Divi, WooCommerce)
- Print trade forums and associations
- Slack/Discord communities for freelancers and small studios
- Referral networks — they get work through word of mouth
- LinkedIn (passively, not posting thought leadership)

### What They Sound Like

- "I just need to get through this week"
- "I can't take on new clients because I'm behind on current ones"
- "I don't have time to learn another tool"
- "My freelancer flaked again"
- "I'm doing work that's below my pay grade but no one else will do it right"
- "I stayed up until 2am resizing banners"

### What They DON'T Sound Like

- "Scale" / "leverage" / "10x" / "systems"
- "AI-powered workflows" / "automation stack"
- "Building in public" / "sharing my journey"
- Any of the bro-culture agency Twitter language

---

## Why This Market Is Good

1. **They already have clients and revenue** — no convincing that work exists
2. **Their backlog is repetitive, specifiable tasks** — banner resizes, page builds, content updates, plugin maintenance, PDF exports — exactly what's automatable or outsourceable
3. **They're not looking for AI — they're looking for relief** — sell the outcome, not the tool
4. **Low competition for attention** — nobody is marketing to these people because they're not flashy
5. **They pay for results** — not for "innovation" or "transformation"
6. **High retention** — once you clear backlog, they'll keep sending work
7. **Trust-based buying** — they buy from people they know, referrals, proven track record

## What the Playbook Needs to Cover

### 1. Messaging Validation (Click Testing)

Run the Steve Pieper click test method to find which pain points resonate before spending on real campaigns. Test pain point hooks like:

- "My backlog never actually clears"
- "I can't find reliable freelancers"
- "I'm doing work below my pay grade"
- "I don't have time to learn AI tools"
- "I'm losing clients to shops that move faster"
- "I stayed up until 2am doing production work"

See: `_planning/steve-pieper-click-test-method-v2.md`

### 2. Language & Positioning

Don't sell AI. Don't sell systems. Sell:

- Backlog cleared
- Work done right, on time
- No hiring, no managing, no training
- Overflow handled so they can focus on clients and new business
- A team they don't have to manage

Avoid: scale, leverage, AI-powered, automation, 10x, disrupt, transform

### 3. Where to Reach Them

- Facebook ads targeting WordPress/web design interests + small business owner demographics
- WordPress community sponsorships or meetup presence
- Print trade association directories
- Local business group referrals
- Cold outreach via LinkedIn (not spammy — value-first)
- Google Ads for "freelance web designer overflow" / "WordPress maintenance help" type searches

### 4. Foot-in-the-Door Offer

The first engagement needs to be:

- Low risk for them
- Immediately useful
- Demonstrates competence on THEIR type of work
- Small enough to say yes without a meeting

Ideas:

- "Send us 5 tasks from your backlog. We'll knock out 2 for free."
- Fixed-price backlog sprint — clear X items in Y days
- WordPress maintenance audit — "here's what's broken across your sites"
- One-time banner/asset production batch

### 5. Delivery Workflow

How we actually do the work. Needs documenting:

- Intake process (how do they send us work?)
- Task types we handle (and don't handle)
- Turnaround expectations
- QA process
- Communication cadence
- Tools we use internally (AI-assisted but they don't need to know or care)

### 6. Pricing Models

- **Per-task** — simple, transparent, for one-off overflow
- **Backlog sprint** — fixed price for a batch of items cleared in a set timeframe
- **Monthly retainer** — ongoing overflow capacity, X hours/tasks per month
- **Dedicated resource** — "your designer/developer, on call, without the hiring"

### 7. Scaling the Service

- What can be automated vs what needs human craft
- When to hire vs when to use AI tooling
- Quality control at volume
- Client communication templates
- Onboarding playbook for new clients

---

## Anti-Patterns to Avoid

- Don't become the thing we're avoiding — don't post about "systems" all day
- Don't oversell AI — the client doesn't care HOW the work gets done
- Don't chase the "agency" identity — we're a production partner, not a brand agency
- Don't price like a freelancer — price like a solution
- Don't take on strategy/consulting work early — stay in execution lane until established
- Don't build a complex funnel before validating messaging with click tests
